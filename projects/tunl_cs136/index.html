Nuclear reactions @ TUNL

Charge-exchange experiments to study the nuclear structure of 136Cs

Photo of the beamline and 136Xe target chamber at TUNL. The target is surrounded by four high-purity germanium (HPGe) detectors for the detection of gamma rays. (Photo: Sean Finch)


The nuclear level structure of 136Cs is of interest for two major reasons:

  1. 136Cs is the intermediate nucleus in the double-beta-decay of 136Xe. It can therefore be used to benchmark nuclear models, which are the leading source theoretical uncertainty in the interpretation of 0νββ experimental results. In particular, low-lying excited states in 136Cs can contribute to sub-leading terms in the calculation of matrix elements for both 0νββ and 2νββ in 136Xe, and therefore are of fundamental importance for experiments like nEXO.

  2. Charged-current reactions on 136Xe nuclei will produce excited 136Cs nuclei in the final state, which would then emit characteristic gamma rays which could be used to tag these rare interactions in large-scale liquid xenon experiments such as LZ or nEXO. Of particular interest: this reaction is sensitive to the neutrinos produced by the solar CNO cycle, which provide information on the abundance of heavy elements (specifically carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen) in the sun’s core. New measurements may help solve the long-standing tension between different models of the solar composition (the so-called “solar metallicity problem”). Furthermore, there are theories of dark matter that predict new particles which undergo similar absorption reactions (i.e., 136Xe + χ → 136Cs + e-). My collaborators and I have shown that, with dedicated analyses to search for 136Cs decays, next-generation liquid xenon TPCs may be uniquely sensitive to these signals (PhysRevD.102.072009).

Despite its importance, there are only a handful of measurements of 136Cs to date, and its nuclear properties remain largely unknown. To remedy this, we are engaged in a series of experiments at Triangle Universities Nuclear Laboratory (TUNL) in Durham, NC that will characterterize the level scheme and relaxation of excited states in 136Cs. We bombard a 136Xe gas target with protons accelerated to 7 MeV, producing 136Cs via the Xe(p,n)Cs reaction.
